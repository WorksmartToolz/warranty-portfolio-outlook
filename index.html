The warranty portfolio comprises 302 projects across 8 product lines spanning 2017–2025 install dates. Active warranty obligations total 264 projects; 38 projects have no warranty documentation on file representing $33.7M sold material value. The most critical distinction surfaced in this view is between obligation runoff (long-tail racking/foundation, 20–25 year terms) and claim-risk runoff (short-tail component/workmanship, 1–10 year terms) — the two curves diverge by a decade or more for most product lines. For every product line, the short-term warranties (component 5–10 yr, workmanship 1–5 yr) are where claim activity actually concentrates. Click any ⓘ for calculation details.
As of April 8, 2026, Terrasmart has spent $415,763 on warranty claims in 2026 YTD across 190 claims (169 approved, 21 denied), compared to $7,535,164 for the full year 2025 across 411 claims. TerraTrak-2P is the dominant cost driver in both years, representing 78% of 2026 spend. The 2026 YTD figure is expected to be substantially lower than 2025 full-year totals — year-over-year comparisons should be interpreted with caution given that 2026 data covers only a partial year (Q1 + early Q2).

Two runoff curves, not one: Each project tracks up to four warranty types — Racking (10–25 yr), Foundation (10–25 yr), Component (5–10 yr), Workmanship (1–5 yr). The dashboard distinguishes two runoff scenarios:

  • Claim-Risk Active — project still under short-term warranty (component OR workmanship not yet expired). This is where claim volume actually concentrates because short-term warranties cover the components that fail.
  • Obligation Active — project still under long-term warranty (racking OR foundation not yet expired). Terrasmart is still contractually on the hook for these, though claim probability is materially lower.

Why the distinction matters: A product line can be in "claim-risk runoff" years before it's in "obligation runoff." Sunflower is a textbook example — most projects installed 2017–2020, components expire 5 years later (2022–2025), so claim-risk window has already largely closed even though racking obligation runs to ~2028–2030.

Per-active-project claim cost: 2025 total claim spend per product line ÷ count of projects currently in the claim-risk window. Gives a per-project view of typical claim cost using the aggregate data available.

⚠ Reading the Gap

For most product lines, the claim-risk curve falls to near-zero 5–10 years before the obligation curve does. That gap is the "supported but low-exposure" window — useful framing for resource planning, product line strategy, and sunset decision-making.

This view rolls each product line into a single decision-support card. Status classifications reflect a combination of active project count, recent install activity, claim-risk window position, and remaining obligation duration. The Sunflower spotlight panel beneath the scorecards gives the dedicated exit-decision view we discussed. Status definitions — Growing: recent installs (2024+), expanding claim-risk window. Stable: ongoing installs, active claim-risk window. Sunsetting: no recent installs, claim-risk window in active decline. Legacy: discontinued line, residual obligation only. Dormant: minimal portfolio footprint, low ongoing activity.

Sunflower Runoff & Exit Picture

Sunflower represents 149 projects (49% of the portfolio by count), but the claim-risk window for the bulk of those projects has already closed. Most Sunflower installs date to 2017–2020. Components carried 5-year warranties, putting expiration in 2022–2025 — behind us.

2025 claim spend on Sunflower was $2.52M across 120 claims. 2026 YTD has dropped to $45K across 38 claims, an annualized pace of roughly $150K — a 94% reduction year-over-year. This is consistent with a claim-risk window collapsing as predicted.

The obligation tail — racking and foundation warranties — runs through approximately 2034. Terrasmart remains contractually committed to those obligations regardless of any exit decision, but claim probability during that 2026–2034 window will be materially lower than 2017–2025 because the components most likely to fail are no longer under warranty.
